America Is Running Out Of Electricians.

The United States faces a critical shortage of licensed electricians — and the gap is widening every year. With 10,000 retiring annually and only 7,000 entering the field, the crisis is reshaping our infrastructure, economy, and energy future.

The Problem

A Widening Gap

According to the National Electrical Contractors Association, 7,000 new electricians join the industry each year — but 10,000 retire. This net loss of 3,000 skilled workers annually is unsustainable against surging demand driven by clean energy, AI data centers, EV infrastructure, and the largest infrastructure investment in American history.

BlackRock CEO Larry Fink warned at a major energy conference: "We simply do not have enough electricians to meet the growing demand." The electrical workforce is projected to shrink 14% by 2030, while demand could rise by 25% over the same period.

The COVID-19 pandemic halted hands-on apprenticeships just as a generation of Baby Boomer electricians accelerated into retirement — a one-two punch the industry is still recovering from.

Root Causes

Why Is This Happening?

👴

Mass Retirement

Nearly 30% of union electricians are at or near retirement age. As experienced tradespeople leave, the pipeline of replacements simply cannot keep up.

🎓

College-First Culture

For decades, four-year degrees were pushed as the only viable path. Trade schools and apprenticeships were sidelined, shrinking the pool of new entrants to the field.

🦠

COVID-19 Impact

Hands-on apprenticeships were halted by pandemic restrictions. The construction industry lost roughly 430,000 jobs, and only 67% had been recovered by mid-2023.

⚡

Exploding Demand

The Inflation Reduction Act, EV charging networks, AI data centers, and renewable energy are all driving unprecedented new demand for electrical work.
